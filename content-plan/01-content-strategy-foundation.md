# Content Strategy Foundation

This document explains the reasoning behind the 30-day and 90-day content plans. It should be read before choosing daily topics, writing scripts, recording voiceovers, or repurposing posts.

## 1. Core Value Of The Digital Product

Builder's Kit is not just a PDF bundle. Its real value is compression and connection.

The buyer is overwhelmed by scattered business knowledge: books, podcasts, courses, random posts, MBA-style theory, startup frameworks, accounting terms, marketing advice, and advice that only works for one type of company. The kit promises to turn that scattered material into a working mental model of business that can be understood quickly and used immediately.

The paid product should help a builder answer:

- What kind of business am I building?
- What stage am I in?
- What do I need to prove before I commit money, time, or people?
- How do market, customer, offer, pricing, sales, operations, finance, and strategy connect?
- Which concepts matter now, and which can wait?
- How does the logic change for SaaS, services, manufacturing, commerce, marketplaces, or hybrid models?
- What should I do next?

The core promise is: understand the business system in a week, then go back to building with less confusion.

## 2. Core Value Of The Content

The content should be the free daily version of the same promise.

Each daily post or video should give the audience one useful piece of business clarity without making them feel like they need to sit through a course. The content should make business feel plain, connected, and usable.

The content is not random tips. It is not generic book summaries. It is not motivational business posting. It is a public concept map, released one small piece at a time.

The content should help the audience:

- learn one concept quickly
- understand when that concept matters
- see what other business decisions it affects
- avoid one common mistake
- apply the concept to their type of business

If the paid kit is the complete map, the content is the daily zoom-in.

## 3. Strategic Implication

The best content system is not random, and it is not a public course. It should use a hidden editorial architecture.

The audience should be able to discover any post on any day and get value immediately. They should not feel behind, and they should not need to follow the full month in order. Underneath that, the creator should still rotate through a clear map so the feed feels professional, balanced, and connected over time.

The practical rule:

- Public experience: standalone daily business clarity.
- Private system: planned rotation across the business map.
- Long-term effect: the audience slowly realizes every useful post belongs to a larger connected system.

Use three source layers:

| Source | Role In The Content Plan | How To Use It |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Business-development flow | Hidden dependency map | Use as the concept dependency graph and quality-control layer, not as a public syllabus. |
| MBA-style categories | Coverage checklist | Make sure the content covers marketing, HR, operations, finance, accounting, strategy, environment, and management. |
| Famous business books | Recognizable hooks and supporting lenses | Use a few books to explain concept nodes and preview the 30 Books Companion, not as standalone summary content. |

The public audience should feel daily usefulness first. The coherent journey should be visible only in hindsight, not forced like a course.

## 3.1 The Middle Ground

Use a **magazine model**, not a course model.

A good business magazine does not publish all marketing topics one week, all finance topics the next week, and all operations topics after that. It has recurring columns. Readers can enter anywhere. Over time, the publication develops a recognizable worldview.

Builder's Kit content should work the same way:

- One day can be pricing.
- The next day can be operations.
- The next can be a famous book idea.
- The next can be a manufacturing metric.
- The next can be a customer research mistake.

That can feel random to the audience in a good way, as long as it is not random internally. The internal map keeps the quality high.

## 4. The Nine Evergreen Pillars

All content should map to one of these pillars.

1. **Business As A System** - business types, stages, environment, constraints, decision logic, evidence.
2. **Market And Customer Reality** - market research, customer research, industry analysis, competitors, TAM/SAM/SOM.
3. **Offer And Positioning** - problem, segment, value proposition, product/service design, MVP, differentiation.
4. **Business Model And Pricing** - revenue architecture, pricing, packaging, monetization, unit logic.
5. **Marketing, Sales, And Distribution** - channels, sales motions, messaging, funnel, negotiation, retention handoff.
6. **Operations And Delivery** - operating model, process, quality, inventory, production, fulfillment, support.
7. **People And Management** - leadership, communication, org structure, roles, hiring, incentives, management cadence.
8. **Finance, Accounting, And Metrics** - statements, cost structure, unit economics, budgeting, forecasting, KPIs, cash.
9. **Strategy, Risk, And Growth** - moats, resource allocation, compliance, capital, expansion, business plan assembly.

## 5. The Daily Content Rule

Every daily source video should pass this test:

> One concept. One decision. One connection. One application.

That means each video should have:

- a plain concept
- the business decision it helps with
- the downstream connections it affects
- one sector-specific example or translation

The actual script can change later. The planning topic should stay clear before scripting starts.

## 6. Recommended Rotation Rhythm

To avoid both extremes, use a repeating rotation mix. This rhythm is for planning, not something to announce publicly.

| Rotation Slot | Purpose | Example Format |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Core concept | Teach one useful business idea | Pricing, cash flow, positioning, inventory turns. |
| Common mistake | Make the content immediately practical | "Revenue is not cash" or "TAM is not proof." |
| Connection chain | Show how concepts affect each other | Pricing -> margin -> CAC budget -> channel -> hiring. |
| Sector translation | Keep the brand cross-sector | How retention changes in SaaS, services, commerce, and manufacturing. |
| Book/framework/case lens | Add memorability and authority | Lean Startup for evidence loops, Blue Ocean for positioning, StoryBrand for messaging. |
| Operator question | Invite replies and community learning | "What part of your business breaks first when volume increases?" |

The feed can jump between topics. The important thing is that every 7-10 posts include a healthy mix of these slots.

## 7. Repurposing System

Each daily video is the source. Other formats come from it.

| Format | Use |
| --- | --- |
| 30-60 second 2D animated video | Main daily source piece for all video platforms. |
| Short written post | X, LinkedIn, Facebook, and community posting. |
| WhatsApp message | One plain takeaway and one useful question, especially for business groups. |
| Blog section or concept page | Bundle related daily topics into a deeper written asset. |
| Weekly Operator Note | Summarize the week through one practical business decision. |
| Lead magnet or kit asset | Convert repeated topic clusters into worksheets, glossary entries, or map nodes. |

## 8. Sector Rotation Rule

The content should not feel like SaaS content with occasional non-SaaS examples. Each month should visibly include:

- SaaS/software
- services and agencies
- manufacturing and physical production
- commerce, retail, and products
- marketplaces and platforms
- hybrid businesses

Use SaaS metrics only when SaaS is the topic. For other sectors, translate the logic properly.

## 9. Book And Framework Rule

Books are useful, but they should not become the content architecture.

Book-hook videos are useful in small doses because famous books give the audience an easy entry point into the Builder's Kit worldview. They also show that the kit contains a deeper 30 Books Companion. The risk is that the brand becomes a generic summary account, so every book video must still be mapped to a business decision.

Use this rule for every book-hook video:

- one famous book
- one main lesson
- one business decision it helps with
- one Builder's Kit concept node it connects to
- one practical example or warning
- one light CTA toward the 30 Books Companion, newsletter, or concept library

Do not summarize every chapter. Do not rely on long quotations. Do not make the video a substitute for the book. The video should say: "Here is the working lesson, and here is where it fits in the business system."

Use books this way:

- **The Lean Startup** - evidence, experiments, MVP, learning loops.
- **Blue Ocean Strategy** - positioning, category choice, strategic differentiation.
- **Zero to One** - defensibility, monopoly logic, unique advantage.
- **Never Split The Difference** - sales conversations, negotiation, listening, objection handling.
- **The One Page Marketing Plan** - GTM structure, channel logic, campaign simplicity.
- **Building A StoryBrand** - messaging, customer problem, promise, proof.
- **The SaaS Playbook** - SaaS-specific growth and metrics, clearly labeled as not universal.

The rule: every book-derived topic must connect to a Builder's Kit concept node.

## 10. CTA Posture

For the first 30 days, the audience should feel taught, not pitched.

Use this posture:

- Primary CTA: free concept library, glossary, worksheet, email list, or newsletter.
- Secondary CTA: invite replies, questions, and examples from their businesses.
- Avoid direct kit selling during the first 30 days unless the launch calendar has already moved into waitlist mode.

For days 31-60, introduce the waitlist gently.

For days 61-90, connect social proof, kit previews, and launch CTAs back to the same concept map.

## 11. Quality Rules

- Do not publish isolated definitions without application.
- Do not publish generic book summaries.
- Do not use metric dumps without owner, cadence, and decision rule.
- Do not make SaaS the default business model.
- Do not turn every post into a pitch.
- Do not talk down to beginners.
- Do not use hype, fake scarcity, or guru framing.
- Always show what changes by business type or stage when that matters.
