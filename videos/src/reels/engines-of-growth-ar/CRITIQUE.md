# Engines of Growth — Critique

## Core Diagnosis

The first version explains the right framework, but it feels like an advertisement because it moves through polished branded-looking assets faster than it teaches. The viewer sees labels, chips, logos, and gauges, but does not get enough time or visual cause-and-effect to understand what each engine actually means.

The revision should become a teaching video, not a promotional montage. Every engine needs to answer four viewer questions in order:

1. What is this engine?
2. What exactly is the input?
3. What exactly is the output?
4. What breaks if the inequality is false?

## Problems From The Current Video

### 1. The pacing is too fast

The video moves from framework to sticky to viral to paid to trap to diagnostic in about two minutes. That can work only if each visual is instantly legible. Here, the visuals require interpretation, so the speed makes the lesson feel thin.

The terms are introduced before the viewer has a mental model:

- `STICKY` appears before retention/churn are explained.
- `VIRAL` appears before K is defined in plain language.
- `PAID` appears before CAC and LTV are defined.
- The trap scene assumes the viewer already understands all three engines.

### 2. The sticky bucket metaphor is under-explained

The bucket scene shows a tap and bottom leaks, but the viewer does not yet know what they represent. The narration says "tap on top" and "leaks at the bottom," but those are metaphor labels, not business labels.

The screen needs to explicitly map metaphor to business reality:

- Tap = new customers joining this month.
- Water level = active paying customers.
- Leaks = customers cancelling or churning.
- Calendar bills = recurring revenue from customers who stay.
- Churn = future recurring payments disappearing.

The current scene also says "Spotify. Your gym. Retention." too quickly. That feels like a name-drop, not an explanation. The examples need to be embedded in the mechanism: monthly payment, customer stays, customer cancels, future months vanish.

### 3. The viral scene explains spread, but not the business model

The viral scene shows invitations and a K dial, but it does not make the goal clear enough: viral businesses need a large audience because the audience is what creates value.

A first-time viewer needs to see:

- Users invite users.
- If each user brings more than one new user on average, the audience grows without buying every user.
- Some platforms do not charge the users directly.
- The platform sells audience attention to advertisers, creators, sellers, or another paying side.
- If the audience does not grow, there is nothing valuable to sell.

The current scene jumps to "audience is the product" without giving enough setup. It should first show a small empty audience, then a growing audience, then the paying side arriving because the audience exists.

### 4. Paid growth does not define CAC and LTV clearly enough

The paid scene says "spend thirty" and "pay back ninety," but CAC and LTV are never unpacked at the right moment.

The viewer needs clear definitions on screen:

- CAC = Customer Acquisition Cost = money spent on ads, sales calls, commissions, promotions, or marketing to get one new customer.
- LTV = Lifetime Value = all gross profit or revenue that customer produces over their relationship with the business.
- Paid growth works only when LTV is larger than CAC.
- If CAC is larger than LTV, buying more customers buys more losses.

The current visual looks like a coin machine, which reads as flashy and ad-like. A better visual is a simple ledger: money spent to acquire, customer arrives, money earned over time, profit/loss line.

### 5. The trap scene is too compressed

The trap scene has three rows, but each row relies on labels the viewer may not have internalized yet. The visual rows are also busy and small.

Each failure mode should be stated as a simple sentence:

- Subscription with high churn: ads pour in customers, but customers cancel before enough months pass.
- Social/audience business with no sharing: paid acquisition creates isolated users, not a network effect or audience.
- Product with bad unit economics: viral demand multiplies a negative margin.

The trap should not feel like a recap slide. It should feel like three quick, concrete failure stories.

### 6. The diagnostic scene asks good questions, but not slowly enough

"Which engine is yours?" should be the practical payoff, but it currently runs like a quiz animation.

A viewer needs a calm diagnostic:

- If customers pay repeatedly, first check sticky: do enough stay?
- If users do not pay but attention/audience creates money, first check viral: does each user bring more users?
- If customers usually buy once or need to be acquired one at a time, first check paid: does each acquired customer return more value than the acquisition cost?

The diagnostic should use simple question cards and one clear example at a time, not several avatars moving at once.

## Visual And Animation Problems

### 1. Text is too small for a reel

Many labels are legible in a desktop preview but not on a phone. Examples:

- Pedestal sublabels (`RETENTION`, `UNIT ECONOMICS`, inequalities) are too small.
- Sticky example chips at the bottom are tiny and close to the edge.
- Calendar month labels and `$10` stamps are small.
- Trap row reason text is too small.

Revision rule: any primary teaching text should be 52px or larger. Secondary labels should usually be 34px or larger. Tiny decorative labels should be removed, not kept.

### 2. Too many simultaneous elements

Several scenes ask the viewer to read a title, a diagram, a calendar, a counter, examples, and a persistent spine at the same time. The persistent spine is conceptually helpful but visually expensive.

Revision rule: the top spine should be simplified to a small progress strip, not full pedestals. The main teaching area should own most of the frame.

### 3. Overlap and overflow

The stills show cropped/overflowing elements:

- Sticky calendar extends too far right.
- Sticky chips sit too low and near the edge.
- Trap rows fit after patching, but the underlying design still uses cramped side-by-side cards.
- Some labels compete with illustrations instead of being paired with them.

Revision rule: use fewer elements per scene, larger safe margins, and one diagram per beat.

### 4. The person SVG feels crude

The pushing figure and customer shapes read as placeholder graphics. They make the video feel less deliberate.

Revision rule: use abstract but polished human tokens: circular head, rounded torso, clear motion arcs, and labels where needed. Avoid awkward arms or tiny details.

### 5. Brand/logo feel makes the lesson feel salesy

Logo-like avatars and example chips make the reel feel like a promo deck. The topic is conceptual, so examples should be concrete but not brand-advertising.

Revision rule: use neutral example cards: `STREAMING SUBSCRIPTION`, `GYM MEMBERSHIP`, `SOCIAL APP`, `MATTRESS STORE`, `T-SHIRT SHOP`. Use named categories and numbers rather than mimicking logos.

### 6. The metaphors need labels at the moment they appear

The viewer cannot be asked to infer business meaning from metaphor objects. When a tap appears, label it `NEW CUSTOMERS`. When water rises, label it `ACTIVE PAYING CUSTOMERS`. When a leak appears, label it `CHURN / CANCELS`.

### 7. The current structure compresses definition and example into one scene

Sticky, viral, and paid each try to define the engine, show the example, and close with formal vocabulary in one scene. That is why the scenes feel rushed.

Revision rule: each engine gets two beats inside its scene:

- Plain definition with labeled mechanics.
- Concrete example with the inequality becoming true or false.

## Better Representation Principles

1. Teach with labeled cause-and-effect, not decorative symbols.
2. Use neutral examples instead of brand-like logos.
3. Define the acronym before using it as a gauge.
4. Keep one main diagram on screen at a time.
5. Make failure visible with arithmetic, not just red styling.
6. Use large typography and fewer captions.
7. Make the viewer answer the question: "What must be bigger than what?"
8. If a scene uses a metaphor, show the translation next to it.

## Acceptance Test For The Revision

After one watch, a first-time viewer should be able to answer:

1. What does sticky growth mean?
2. What is churn?
3. Why does churn destroy future months, not just today?
4. What does viral growth need besides sharing?
5. What does K mean?
6. Why does an audience business need a large audience?
7. What does CAC mean?
8. What does LTV mean?
9. Why does LTV need to be bigger than CAC?
10. Why can the wrong engine make a business worse?
11. How do I choose which engine to focus on first?

If the revised reel cannot pass this test, the plan is still not ready.
